Daily Reflection Tree — Design Rationale
1. Objective

The goal of this reflection system is to guide an employee through a deterministic, structured introspection process across three psychological axes:

Axis 1: Locus (Victim ↔ Victor) — awareness of agency
Axis 2: Orientation (Entitlement ↔ Contribution) — focus on giving vs receiving
Axis 3: Radius (Self ↔ Others) — expansion of perspective

The system is designed to surface patterns of thinking, not evaluate or judge behavior.

2. Design Philosophy

The core design principle was:

Reflection should feel like a conversation, not a survey.

To achieve this, I avoided:

Generic or vague options
Emotion-heavy language without behavioral grounding
Overlapping choices

Instead, I focused on:

Concrete behaviors (e.g., “took initiative” vs “waited”)
Mutually exclusive options
Progressive insight building across axes
3. Tree Structure

Each axis follows a consistent 3-stage structure:

Entry Question → Behavioral Question → Reflection

Additionally:

Decision nodes route based on user responses
Signals accumulate to determine dominant patterns
Bridges connect axes to maintain conversational flow

This ensures:

Determinism (no ambiguity)
Readability (clear branching)
Psychological progression (not fragmented questioning)
4. Question Design

Questions were designed to:

Trigger recognition rather than opinion
Capture real workplace scenarios
Avoid socially desirable bias

Example:

Instead of:

“Did you take responsibility?”

I used:

“During a key moment, how did you respond?”

Options:

Took initiative
Waited for clarity
Adjusted strategy
Avoided the issue

This forces the user to map themselves to observable behavior.

5. Branching Strategy

Branching is based on:

Immediate responses (via decision nodes)
Accumulated signals (internal vs external, etc.)

Three reflection types are used:

Reinforcing (internal/contribution/wide)
Reframing (external/entitlement/self)
Neutral awareness (mixed states)

This avoids moralizing while still nudging insight.

6. Psychological Foundations

The tree is grounded in established frameworks:

Locus of Control — Julian Rotter (1954)
→ Operationalized through perceived control in responses
Growth Mindset — Carol Dweck (2006)
→ Embedded in adaptability vs stuck responses
Organizational Citizenship Behavior — Organ (1988)
→ Captured through discretionary contribution
Self-Transcendence — Abraham Maslow (1969)
→ Represented through widening radius of concern
7. Trade-offs

Key trade-offs made:

Depth vs Complexity
→ Limited branching to keep the tree interpretable
Precision vs Coverage
→ Focused on strong signals rather than exhaustive scenarios
Determinism vs Personalization
→ Used interpolation instead of generative text
8. Improvements with More Time
Introduce multi-day memory to track patterns over time
Add context-specific trees (manager vs individual contributor)
Refine summary node with richer interpolation logic
Conduct user testing to eliminate ambiguous options
9. Key Insight

The most important realization during this design was:

The quality of reflection is determined not by answers, but by the precision of the options provided.

Designing those options is the core of knowledge engineering.
